The Office

The Office (US) is the definitive mockumentary that turned the mundane boredom of a mid-sized paper company into a masterpiece of cringe and heart. It thrives on the awkwardness of Michael Scott, a desperate-to-be-loved manager, and the grounded, "will-they-won't-they" romance of Jim and Pam. Unlike traditional sitcoms, its lack of a laugh track and its use of "talking head" interviews create an intimate, fly-on-the-wall feeling. It starts as a satire of corporate soul-crushing but eventually evolves into a warm tribute to the idea that "even an ordinary paper company can be extraordinary."

Friends

Friends is the quintessential "multi-cam" sitcom that defined a generation by shifting the focus from the traditional nuclear family to the family you choose in your twenties. Set in an impossibly large Manhattan apartment and a cozy coffee shop, it follows six distinct personalities—Rachel, Monica, Phoebe, Joey, Chandler, and Ross—as they navigate the messy transition into adulthood. While The Office finds humor in the realistic and the awkward, Friends leans into polished, rhythmic banter and iconic catchphrases. It’s a show about that specific time in life when your friends are your world before "real life" (marriage, kids, careers) takes over.

Brooklyn nine nine

Brooklyn Nine-Nine takes the workplace setting of The Office and the tight-knit ensemble of Friends, then injects them with a shot of high-energy optimism and police procedural tropes. It follows Jake Peralta, a talented but immature detective, and his relationship with the stoic, robotic Captain Raymond Holt. What sets this show apart is its "no-bully" comedy; the humor comes from the characters' quirks and mutual respect rather than meanness. It’s fast-paced, inclusive, and famous for its cold opens and annual "Halloween Heist" episodes, which actually bridge the gap back to the "mastermind" vibe of your first list.
